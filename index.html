The Hand Behind the Pen

✦
MH
Muhammad Hamza · Author & Chronicler

Muhammad Hamza

Writer · Dreamer of Victorian Fogs · Keeper of Baker Street's Flame

There is a particular kind of reader who does not merely read a story — who instead enters it, takes up residence in its rooms, learns the smell of its tobacco and the sound of its rain against the glass. Muhammad Hamza is that kind of reader. And, as the chronicles gathered on these pages attest, he is also that kind of writer.

He does not announce himself loudly. He does not explain his obsessions. He simply sits down, somewhere between midnight and the small hours, and begins: It was during the bitter winter of— and the fog rolls in, and somewhere a hansom cab rattles over wet cobblestones, and Sherlock Holmes is leaning against the mantelpiece again, grey eyes alight with the particular fire of a mind that has just seen what no one else has seen.

That Watson narrates these tales is a technicality. That Hamza wrote them is the truth beneath the fiction — the hand behind the pen, the intelligence that devised each crime, each cipher, each revelation, each closing line delivered with a slightly raised eyebrow by the most famous detective in literary history. He gives Watson the voice and Holmes the brilliance, but the imagination belongs entirely to him.

What compels a writer of the twenty-first century to inhabit the nineteenth so completely? Ask him, and he will likely deflect — gesture at Doyle, speak of craft and influence, name books the way other people name old friends. But the answer, if you are paying attention, is simpler and stranger: he loves the world. The gas-lit London of a hundred and thirty years ago, with its particular combination of grandeur and squalor, its confidence and its secret shames, its fogs that permit all manner of wickedness and its brilliant outliers who cut through those fogs like a blade — he loves it with the unreasonable fervour of someone who was, perhaps, born in the wrong century.

On Writing Holmes

Muhammad Hamza on Method, Voice & Obsession

The stories you have read — or are about to read — were written in the belief that the Holmesian form is not merely a historical curiosity but a living instrument, capable of producing new music in hands that respect its construction. Muhammad Hamza brings to these pages a reverence for the original that never collapses into imitation, and an originality that never forgets its debt.

The challenge of writing Holmes afresh is not the plot — plots are abundant, and a good mystery can be engineered from almost any premise. The challenge is voice: specifically, Watson's voice, which is one of the great narrative inventions of English literature. Watson is not a fool playing straight man to a genius. He is a man of considerable intelligence and feeling who happens to be in the company of someone even more extraordinary — and who is generous enough, loyal enough, to make that someone the centre of every room he walks into.

Hamza understands this. His Watson observes with a physician's eye, reflects with a soldier's steadiness, and writes with the quiet, unhurried precision of a man who knows that the truth, if told carefully enough, requires no embellishment. His Holmes is recognisably the Holmes of Baker Street — the mercurial brilliance, the cold exterior, the rare but devastating warmth — while being, in each story, freshly encountered, as though we are meeting him again for the first time.

The crimes he invents are never gratuitous. Each one carries within it a moral weight — old injustice, corrupted genius, the terrible things that people do when they believe their cause is sufficient. He is interested, above all, in the question of what a brilliant mind owes the world in which it operates. It is not, one suspects, an entirely academic question.

The Writer's Library

Sources, Ghosts & the Company He Keeps

Every writer carries a library inside them — not just the books they have read but the ones that have stayed, that altered the furniture of the mind. Muhammad Hamza's library, as these stories make evident, is a particular and revealing one.

At its centre stands Sir Arthur Conan Doyle, whose sixty canonical Holmes stories constitute one of the most sustained achievements of popular literature — works that have never, in more than a century, ceased to engage new readers in new countries. These stories are imitation in the oldest and most respectful sense: the form of tribute that is also a form of study, the way a young painter copies a master not to steal but to understand.

Beside Doyle on the shelf: Wilkie Collins, whose narrative engineering remains technically instructive; Edgar Allan Poe, the grandfather of the detective form, whose shadows fall long over all subsequent practitioners; Thomas Hardy, whose moorland and coastline feel, in these pages, like spiritual cousins; and Joseph Conrad, whose understanding of how civilisation conceals its own darkness has clearly informed the moral atmosphere Hamza builds so carefully around each case.

And then, perhaps most importantly, the Victorian periodical itself — the Strand Magazine, the illustrated weeklies, the serials that were delivered to households across England and read aloud by firelight. The presentation of a story, these editors understood, is itself a kind of argument about what stories are for. Hamza has built these pages in that spirit: a place where literature is not merely consumed but encountered, as though arriving through a letterbox on a grey November morning, with all the promise that such arrivals contain.
